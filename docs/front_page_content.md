
# Why 40 Gy in 10 fractions for palliative thoracic RT?

## Executive Summary
This planning challenge explores the dosimetric realities of escalating palliative thoracic radiotherapy to **40 Gy in 10 fractions** for patients with locally advanced or oligometastatic NSCLC. While aiming for durable local control in tumours often unsuitable for SBRT due to size (>5 cm) or ultracentral location, this regimen significantly increases the biologically effective dose compared to standard 30 Gy in 10 fraction courses [1]. The goal of this exercise is to train the VMAT planning discipline required to safely deliver this escalated dose—balancing target coverage against critical OAR constraints (spinal cord, oesophagus) and maintaining strict lung low-dose limits to minimise toxicity risks [1].

## Evidence snapshot
- **Local control:** 96.7% at 1 year; 76.9% at 3 years [1].
- **Response rate:** 69.3% overall response (RECIST 1.1) [1].
- **Toxicity:** Grade ≥3 oesophagitis occurred in only 1.6% (1/62) of patients; no Grade ≥3 pneumonitis was observed [1].
- **Technique:** 87.1% of patients were treated using VMAT [1].
- **Follow-up:** Median follow-up was 4.1 years [1].

## Where 40/10 sits on the palliative spectrum
This regimen offers a significantly higher biological dose than conventional palliative schedules [1].

| Regimen | Fractions | Total Dose | BED10 (Gy) | EQD2 (Gy) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Single fraction** | 1 | 10 Gy | 20.0 | 16.7 |
| **Standard palliative** | 5 | 20 Gy | 28.0 | 23.3 |
| **High-dose palliative** | 10 | 30 Gy | 39.0 | 32.5 |
| **Escalated palliative** | 10 | 40 Gy | **56.0** | **46.7** |

*BED10 = nd(1 + d/(α/β)) and EQD2 = D(d + α/β)/(2 + α/β) with α/β = 10 Gy.* [1]

## Why technique matters (VMAT)
- **SBRT ineligible:** 85.4% of the study cohort were unsuitable for SBRT due to large tumour size (median PTV 168.8 cm³) or ultracentral location [1].
- **Esophageal sparing:** The single Grade 3 esophagitis event occurred where the tumour abutted the oesophagus (Dmax 42.4 Gy); conformal planning allows prioritisation of sparing in such overlap regions [1].
- **OAR tension:** Delivering 40 Gy requires managing tension between PTV coverage and PRV limits (cord, oesophagus) that 3DCRT may struggle to resolve without compromising coverage [1].
- **Safety profile:** Despite 83.4% of patients having ECOG performance status ≥2, the regimen demonstrated a favourable safety profile when adhering to strict OAR constraints [1].

## What this challenge trains
- **Target coverage discipline** under OAR tension (prioritising safe delivery over perfect coverage if needed).
- **Critical safety gates** validation (hard constraints on Spinal Cord and Cord PRV).
- **Lung low-dose bath awareness** (monitoring V4.5Gy and V16Gy as proxies for pneumonitis risk).
- **Oesophagus sparing awareness** (limiting mean dose and hotspots in overlap regions).
- **Consistency** via an objective scorecard approach to plan evaluation.

## Limitations and cautions
- **Retrospective data:** Evidence is drawn from a retrospective cohort study; adverse events may be underreported compared to prospective trials [1].
- **Patient selection:** The study population was predominantly elderly (median 72.6 years) with poor performance status; results reflect this specific demographic [1].
- **Educational only:** This tool and rubric are for training planning skills only and do not replace local clinical protocols or MDT decision-making.
